## Part A — Executive Brief `{All}`

**Financiar** (codename: Spendly v4) is a multi-tenant fintech SaaS for individuals and small-to-medium businesses, delivering expense tracking, bill payments, payroll, virtual cards, multi-currency wallets, invoicing, vendor management, team collaboration, and AI-assisted financial insights through a unified web and mobile experience.

### What the product does today

- **Web + mobile**: 36 web pages, 22 mobile screens (Android, Google Play internal track at 2026-04-26; iOS pending)
- **Payments**: Dual processor — Stripe for global, Paystack for 10 African countries (NG, GH, KE, ZA, TZ, RW, SN, UG, ZM, ZW)
- **Identity**: AWS Cognito + multi-tier KYC (Stripe Identity, Paystack BVN, manual)
- **Surface area**: ~517 API endpoints, 42 database tables, 4 atomic financial operations (bill payment, wallet transfer, card funding, reversal)
- **Compliance posture**: Webhook signature verification, idempotency at DB level, partial PII redaction, audit logs, encrypted secrets at rest

### Why now

Three forces line up:

1. **Africa's SMB digital-finance shift.** Paystack's 2025 throughput data shows accelerating SMB adoption of digital payments, but tooling above the rails (expense management, multi-currency treasury, invoicing) lags well behind US/EU equivalents. Financiar fills that gap as an SMB OS layered on Paystack.
2. **The "global SMB" is a real persona.** Increasing numbers of African founders bill US/EU customers and pay local employees — a single-rail product (US-only Stripe or Africa-only Paystack) cannot serve them. Financiar's dual-processor architecture is its core moat.
3. **AI-native expense classification** has matured to where deterministic categorisation, anomaly detection, and forecasting can be shipped at low marginal cost. Financiar's `business_insights` and `analytics_snapshots` infrastructure is ready to host that.

### What we want from this document

- **Investors:** a clear picture of the market, the moat, the unit economics, and the funding ask.
- **Leadership:** a shared definition of where the product is, what's contractually committed, what's still ahead, and the operating cadence to execute.
- **Regulatory:** a defensible record of which licences and certifications we hold, which we are pursuing, and on what timeline — sized to the markets we operate in.

### B.2 Pricing model

**Recommendation:** four-tier subscription + transaction-fee mark-up, biased to Africa-first elasticity.

| Tier | Audience | Monthly price | Transaction-fee mark-up | Headline features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Free** | Curious users, evaluators | $0 (₦0) | None — pass-through | 1 user, 1 company, 50 tx/mo cap, basic reports |
| **Starter** | Solo entrepreneurs, freelancers | $9 / ₦4,500 | +0.4% on processed volume | 1 user, 1 company, unlimited tx, virtual cards, basic invoicing |
| **Pro** | SMB (5–25 employees) | $49 / ₦24,500 | +0.4% on volume | Up to 10 users, multi-currency, payroll, KYC, advanced reports, priority support |
| **Enterprise** | Larger SMBs, scaling teams | $199+ / ₦99,000+ or custom | Negotiated | Unlimited users, multi-company, custom roles, SSO (planned), dedicated CSM, 99.9% SLA |

Pricing rationale:

- **Africa elasticity**: NGN price set at parity to global ($1 ≈ ₦500 assumed at this price card). For NG specifically, ₦4,500/mo competes well with QuickBooks Africa, Bumpa, Sycle.
- **Volume mark-up**: a 40-bp mark-up on transaction volume captures upside as customers grow without forcing them to upgrade tiers. Transparent to customer; positioned as "platform fee".
- **Free tier**: meaningful but capped — 50 transactions/mo lets evaluators feel the product but does not cover real operating use.

### B.3 Revenue model

Two revenue streams:

#### B.3.1 Subscription (recurring)

`MRR_subscription = Σ (customers_in_tier × price_per_tier)`

#### B.3.2 Transaction fee (variable)

`MRR_txn = (GMV_processed × markup_rate) - (passthrough_cost_to_processor)`

For a Pro customer processing $50,000/mo: subscription $49 + ($50,000 × 0.4%) = $49 + $200 = $249/mo. Stripe takes ~2.9% + $0.30 per tx, Paystack takes 1.5% + ₦100 (capped at ₦2,000) — net spread ≈ 0.4% accrues to Financiar.

### D.1 Per-country regulatory posture

Snapshot for each active payment market. Status today reflects the most permissive interpretation (relying on Paystack/Stripe as the regulated party). For deposit-taking, lending, or holding customer funds beyond settlement-cycle windows, additional licences are usually required.

| Country | Regulator | Service Financiar performs | Posture today | Direct licence likely needed when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nigeria** | CBN, SEC, NDPC | Payments collection (via Paystack), expense management | Paystack is licensed (PSSP); Financiar is a customer | Holding customer funds beyond settlement window; offering virtual NUBAN at scale → CBN PSSP / Switching licence |
| **Ghana** | Bank of Ghana, NCA | Payments via Paystack | Paystack-licensed | E-money issuer or Payment Service Provider category 2 if holding balances |
| **Kenya** | Central Bank of Kenya, ODPC | Payments via Paystack | Paystack-licensed | Money Remittance licence if cross-border; PSP if domestic |
| **South Africa** | SARB, FSCA, IR | Payments via Paystack | Paystack-licensed | Authorised dealer in foreign exchange (ADLA category 1 or 2) for FX |
| **Tanzania, Rwanda, Senegal, Uganda, Zambia, Zimbabwe** | Country central banks | Payments via Paystack | Paystack-licensed where Paystack operates | Country-specific PSP / e-money licences required for direct operations |
| **United States** | FinCEN, state regulators (NY DFS, CA, etc.) | Payments via Stripe; potential expense management for US SMBs | Stripe-licensed | MSB registration with FinCEN; state-by-state Money Transmitter licences for holding funds (very high cost) |
| **United Kingdom** | FCA | Payments via Stripe | Stripe-licensed | Authorised Payment Institution (API) or Electronic Money Institution (EMI) for direct operations |
| **EU (multiple)** | ECB, national authorities | Payments via Stripe | Stripe-licensed | EMI passportable licence (e.g. via Lithuania or Ireland) for direct operations |
| **Australia** | ASIC, AUSTRAC | Payments via Stripe | Stripe-licensed | AFSL or registration with AUSTRAC |

**Headline guidance:** For 2026 and probably 2027, Financiar can operate as a **technology layer on top of regulated processors**. Direct licensing becomes mandatory the moment Financiar starts custodying customer money beyond the very brief settlement-cycle window or starts moving money outside the processor's covered geography.

### D.4 Data residency

Today: all customer data lives in **AWS us-east-1** (N. Virginia). The CDK stack at `infrastructure/lib/financiar-stack.ts` is single-region.

Implications:

| Jurisdiction | Stance | Action needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Nigeria (NDPR)** | Section 2.13: data must be stored in a country with adequate protection or with explicit consent. The US has been historically accepted as adequate by NDPC subject to safeguards. | Document the data-flow; obtain explicit consent in T&C; verify with counsel. |
| **EU (GDPR)** | Without EU residency, transfers require Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) and a Transfer Impact Assessment | If Financiar onboards EU customers materially, set up an EU-region replica (`eu-central-1` or `eu-west-1`). Likely an EMI-compliance prerequisite anyway. |
| **Kenya (DPA)** | Cross-border transfer requires data-protection assessment | Document the assessment; possibly register with the ODPC. |
| **South Africa (POPIA)** | Allows transfer if recipient is subject to law / binding rules / consent | T&C consent; document. |

Recommendation: stay single-region in 2026, but plan a regional split (NG-region, EU-region, US-region) as part of the Q3 2027 Multi-Region Initiative when SOC 2 audit and EU expansion converge.
